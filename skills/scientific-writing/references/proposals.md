# Government & Commercial Proposals

## Non-Negotiable Rules (apply to ALL proposals)

1. **Mirror the solicitation's section structure exactly.** Never invent your own organization.
2. **Address every evaluation criterion.** Allocate page real estate proportional to criterion weight.
3. **Echo the solicitation's language.** If they say "feedstock-agnostic catalytic technologies," use those exact words.
4. **Include preliminary data.** It is the single strongest predictor of proposal success.
5. **Quantify everything.** "Improved performance" loses to "2.3-fold improvement in titer (45 → 103 g/L)."
6. **State-of-the-art comparison table.** Every technical approach section needs one showing how your approach advances beyond current best.

## Government Proposals (DARPA, ARPA-E, ARPA-H, DOE, BARDA)

Government proposals are the highest-stakes scientific writing in industrial biotech. They fund multi-year, multi-million-dollar programs. The reader is a technical program manager who is simultaneously an expert, a skeptic, and someone evaluating 30-100 submissions.

### Cardinal Rule: Follow the Solicitation

The solicitation (BAA, RFI, NOFO, PS) is the law. Its structure, requirements, page limits, and evaluation criteria are non-negotiable.

- **Mirror the solicitation's section structure exactly.** If they want Technical Approach → Management Plan → Key Personnel → Past Performance, that's your outline. Do not reorganize it.
- **Address every requirement.** Use a compliance matrix if needed. Missing a requirement is often an automatic downgrade.
- **Echo the solicitation's language.** If they say "feedstock-agnostic catalytic technologies," use those exact words when describing your approach. This isn't parroting — it's proving alignment.
- **Respond to evaluation criteria in priority order.** If criteria are ranked (e.g., Technical Approach > Technical Ability > Technical Comprehension), allocate page real estate proportionally.

### DARPA-Specific Conventions

DARPA proposals have a distinct culture:

- **Bold, high-risk/high-reward framing.** DARPA funds what others won't. Highlight what's genuinely novel and transformative, not incremental.
- **Quantitative metrics with clear go/no-go criteria.** DARPA PMs love measurable milestones. "Achieve ≥80 g/L titer across 3 substrate types by Month 8" is excellent.
- **Risk acknowledged and mitigated.** A proposal that claims no risks is not credible. Identify the top 3-5 technical risks, rate their severity/likelihood, and present concrete mitigation strategies.
- **Team capability demonstrated, not just claimed.** Don't say "world-class team." Say "PI has published 23 papers on microbial organic acid production and demonstrated fed-batch succinate fermentation at 500 L scale (Smith et al. 2024)."
- **Brief is better.** DARPA appreciates concision. Dense, substantive paragraphs beat padded sections.

### ARPA-H-Specific Conventions

ARPA-H shares DARPA's bold culture but with a health mission focus:

- **Patient impact front and center.** Frame everything in terms of health outcomes — lives saved, diseases treated, patients reached, time to diagnosis reduced. ARPA-H exists to solve health problems that no one else will tackle.
- **Clinical translation pathway.** Unlike DARPA, ARPA-H expects a clear path from research to clinical use. Address regulatory strategy (FDA pathway), clinical validation, and deployment plan.
- **Health equity considerations.** ARPA-H cares about broad access — address how the technology reaches underserved populations, not just well-resourced health systems.
- **Quantitative health metrics.** Similar to DARPA's go/no-go criteria but health-focused: "Reduce diagnostic turnaround from 14 days to <24 hours" or "Achieve ≥90% sensitivity at <$10/test."
- **Interdisciplinary teams.** ARPA-H values clinical + engineering + regulatory expertise. Show the team spans bench to bedside.

### ARPA-E-Specific Conventions

ARPA-E focuses on transformative energy technologies:

- **Energy impact metrics.** Quantify impact in terms of energy savings, emissions reduction, cost per kWh, or efficiency gains over state-of-the-art.
- **Techno-economic analysis expected.** ARPA-E proposals should include or reference TEA showing a path to cost-competitiveness with incumbent technologies.
- **Scale-up pathway.** Address manufacturing scalability and commercial deployment — ARPA-E wants technologies that can reach market, not just lab demonstrations.
- **Market context.** Show awareness of the competitive landscape: existing solutions, market size, and why the proposed approach wins on cost, performance, or both.

### Proposal Structure (Typical Government)

#### 1. Executive Summary / Overview
- 1 page maximum
- Problem → approach → key innovation → expected impact
- This is the only section every reviewer will definitely read

#### 2. Technical Approach
- The heart of the proposal
- Describe what you will do and how, with enough detail to be falsifiable
- Include preliminary data that de-risks the approach
- Use figures: process flow diagrams, experimental schematics, comparison tables
- Map your approach to program metrics/deliverables explicitly
- **State-of-the-art comparison table**: Show how your approach advances beyond current best results

#### 3. Management/Schedule
- Gantt chart or milestone table
- Phase gates with go/no-go criteria
- Task decomposition (WBS) tied to deliverables
- Show interdependencies between tasks

#### 4. Key Personnel / Team
- Focus on relevant experience, not full CVs
- Highlight prior performance on similar programs
- Explain why each team member is necessary
- For multi-institution teams: show integration plan, not just a list of partners

#### 5. Facilities and Equipment
- Relevant capabilities only
- For biotech: fermentation scale, analytical capabilities, automation, BSL rating
- Don't list everything you own — list what's needed for this program

#### 6. Risk Assessment
- Risk matrix (severity × likelihood)
- Mitigation plan for each significant risk
- Alternative approaches if primary strategy fails

#### 7. Cost / Budget
- Align with technical scope — reviewers check for consistency
- Justify major equipment and travel
- Subcontractor budgets should be proportional to their scope

### Preliminary Data Strategy

Preliminary data is the single strongest predictor of proposal success. It shifts the reviewer's mental model from "will this work?" to "how well will this work at scale?"

**Tiered evidence strategy:**
1. **Published data** (strongest): Peer-reviewed results from your team
2. **Unpublished internal data** (strong): Your own preliminary results, clearly labeled
3. **Published data from others** (moderate): Shows the approach works, but you haven't done it
4. **Computational/theoretical** (weakest but valid): Modeling, DFT calculations, pathway predictions

When presenting preliminary data:
- State clearly whether it's your data or literature
- Include experimental conditions, not just results
- Show reproducibility (error bars, replicates, stability over time)
- Connect each data point to a specific claim or risk reduction

## Commercial / Partnership Proposals

Commercial proposals differ fundamentally from government proposals in audience, tone, and structure.

### Key Differences from Government

| Dimension | Government | Commercial |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Reader | Technical PM | Business development / R&D lead |
| Decision driver | Mission alignment, technical merit | ROI, speed, competitive advantage |
| Tone | Formal, metric-driven | Professional, outcome-focused |
| Risk framing | Acknowledged and mitigated | Minimized but honest |
| Timeline | Phase-gated, milestone-driven | Deliverable-focused, often faster |
| Budget | Justified line-by-line | High-level, value-oriented |

### Commercial Proposal Structure

#### 1. Executive Summary (1 page)
- Lead with the business problem, not the science
- Proposed solution in outcome terms
- Timeline and key deliverables
- Why your organization (2-3 differentiators)

#### 2. Technical Approach
- Focus on outcomes rather than methods
- Include enough science to establish credibility, not enough to overwhelm
- Emphasize de-risking and feasibility evidence
- Comparison to alternative approaches (why this one?)

#### 3. Project Plan
- Phase-gated with clear deliverables
- Decision points where the partner can assess progress
- Realistic timelines with dependencies identified

#### 4. Team and Capabilities
- Relevant experience and facilities
- Similar past projects with outcomes (anonymized if needed)
- Platform capabilities that accelerate the work

#### 5. Commercial Terms
- Budget summary (not line-item unless requested)
- IP treatment (background IP, foreground IP, licensing)
- Payment milestones tied to deliverables

### Writing for Business Audiences

- Lead with "so what" — why should they care?
- Translate scientific concepts: "dynamic pathway regulation" → "our engineered microbes automatically shift their metabolism from growth to production at the optimal time — maximizing output without manual intervention"
- Use bullet points and tables more liberally
- Every paragraph should answer an implicit question the reader has
