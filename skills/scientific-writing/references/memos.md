# Internal Decision Memos

Internal memos in biotech R&D are decision documents, not status updates. They exist to drive a specific decision by presenting options, trade-offs, and a clear recommendation.

## When to Write a Memo

- A technical decision needs alignment across functions (R&D, engineering, commercial, IP)
- An experiment or program is at a go/no-go point
- Resource allocation or prioritization needs justification
- A strategic pivot requires documented reasoning

## Structure

### Subject Line
State the **decision**, not the topic:
- **Weak:** "Strain selection update"
- **Strong:** "Recommendation: Advance C. glutamicum over E. coli for itaconate production"

### Context (2-3 sentences)
Why this memo exists. What triggered the decision point. Who is affected.

### The Decision Needed
State it explicitly with a deadline:
"By [date], we need to decide [X] in order to [Y]."

### Options with Trade-offs

Present as a comparison table:

| Criterion | Option A | Option B | Option C |
|-----------|----------|----------|----------|
| Timeline  | 6 months | 9 months | 4 months |
| Cost      | $250K    | $180K    | $400K    |
| Risk      | Medium   | Low      | High     |
| Upside    | Moderate | Moderate | High     |

For each option, include:
- What it involves (1-2 sentences)
- Key advantage
- Key risk or limitation
- Dependencies and assumptions

### Recommendation

State your recommendation clearly and explain why:
- Which option and why
- What risks you're accepting
- What you'd need to revisit the decision

### Next Steps

Numbered action items with owners and dates:
1. [Action] — [Owner] — [Date]
2. [Action] — [Owner] — [Date]

## Tone and Length

- **Length:** 1-2 pages maximum. If it needs more, it's a report.
- **Tone:** Direct, data-driven, no hedging on the recommendation. You can hedge on uncertainty, but not on your judgment.
- **Audience:** Assume the reader is technically literate but hasn't been tracking this specific issue closely.

## Common Mistakes

- Writing a status update instead of a decision document
- Presenting options without a recommendation (that's abdicating, not advising)
- Burying the decision in background context
- Using the memo to lobby rather than analyze — present trade-offs honestly
- Omitting the "what could go wrong" for the recommended option
