# Research Presentations

Presentations are the most public and most compressed form of scientific communication. A 20-minute talk or a poster viewed for 5 minutes must convey what a 50-page report takes hours to read. Every element must earn its place.

## Slide Deck Design Principles

### One Idea Per Slide
If you need two bullet points, you might need two slides. Cognitive load is the enemy — the audience processes your voice and the slide simultaneously. Dense slides force them to choose between reading and listening.

### Figures Over Text
The best scientific slides contain a single figure with a clear title and minimal annotation. If you find yourself writing paragraphs on a slide, rethink the slide.

### Assertion-Evidence Structure
Replace generic titles ("Results") with assertion titles that state the finding:
- **Weak:** "Fed-Batch Fermentation Results"
- **Strong:** "Engineered *E. coli* achieves 146 g/L succinate on crude glycerol at 96% theoretical yield"

The title is the takeaway. The figure is the evidence. The speaker provides the narrative connecting them.

## Deck Structure

### Opening (2-3 slides)
1. **Title slide**: Title, presenter, affiliation, date, program context
2. **The problem**: Why should the audience care? One compelling statistic or image.
3. **The gap**: What can't be done now? Sets up your contribution.

### Technical Core (varies)
- Organize by narrative, not by chronology
- Each slide: assertion title + evidence (figure/table/diagram)
- Build complexity gradually — don't front-load the hardest concepts
- Include a process/pipeline overview slide before diving into details
- Use animation/builds sparingly and purposefully (to reveal data progressively, not for decoration)

### Key Results (2-4 slides)
- Headline findings with the strongest data
- Comparison to benchmarks or program targets
- These slides get photographed and shared — make them self-contained

### Conclusions and Next Steps (1-2 slides)
- 3-5 key takeaways, not a summary of every result
- Next steps with specificity
- The ask (if any): funding, collaboration, feedback

### Acknowledgments (1 slide)
- Funding sources with grant numbers
- Collaborators and team members
- Required disclaimers

## Presentation Types

### Conference Talk (15-25 min)
- 1 slide per 1.5-2 minutes
- Focus on 2-3 key results, not comprehensive coverage
- Prepare for 5-10 minutes of questions

### Program Review (30-60 min)
- More comprehensive, milestone-focused
- Include progress tables mapping to SOW
- Show technical detail AND strategic context
- Budget status if expected
- Program managers want to see: what you did, what you found, what it means, what's next

### Lightning Talk / Elevator Pitch (3-5 min)
- 3-5 slides maximum
- Problem → approach → one key result → significance
- Rehearse to the second — going over time is unprofessional

### Poster
- Read in 3-5 minutes standing 3 feet away
- Title must be readable from 6 feet
- Flow: left-to-right, top-to-bottom OR clearly numbered sections
- Figures should be self-explanatory with detailed captions
- Include QR code linking to paper/preprint if available
- Contact info prominently displayed

## Speaking Notes

For each slide, prepare (mentally or in notes):
1. **Transition sentence** connecting to the previous slide
2. **Key point** — the one thing the audience must understand from this slide
3. **Supporting detail** — elaboration if time allows
4. **Anticipated question** — what might the audience ask?

## Common Presentation Pitfalls

- **Too many slides.** Rushing through slides is worse than having fewer, well-paced ones.
- **Reading the slides.** If you're reading, the audience is reading ahead, not listening.
- **No narrative arc.** A collection of results is not a presentation. Every slide should answer "so what?"
- **Illegible figures.** Test figures at presentation resolution. If you can't read axis labels from the back of the room, redo them.
- **Burying the lead.** Put your most important result in the first third, not the last slide before conclusions.
- **No practice.** One full rehearsal at pace catches 80% of issues.

## Real-World Presentation Patterns (Sanitized Examples)

### Pattern: Commercial/Partnership Proposal Decks

Client-facing proposal presentations (for commercial partnerships, not government) follow a distinct structure optimized for business decision-makers:

```
Slide 1: Title — "[Company] x [Partner] [Service/Product] Proposal [Date]"
Slide 2: The Opportunity — Partner's problem stated in their language
Slide 3: Our Approach — 1-2 sentence value proposition + technical summary
Slide 4: Technical Strategy — Pipeline/workflow diagram (visual, minimal text)
Slides 5-8: Capabilities Evidence — Past results, platform descriptions, 
            benchmarks (each slide = one assertion + one figure)
Slide 9: Proposed Scope — What we'll do, what we'll deliver, timeline
Slide 10: Team — Key personnel with relevant expertise (photos + 1 line each)
Slide 11: Next Steps — Specific actions, timeline, contact info
```

Key differences from academic/government presentations:
- **No methods detail** — the partner cares about outcomes, not how you do it
- **Outcomes framed as business value** — "3-month faster timeline" not "optimized expression system"
- **The partner's name appears throughout** — this is about THEM, not you
- **Budget is NOT in the deck** — discussed separately (never put pricing in slides that get forwarded)

### Pattern: Conference Abstract → Slide Deck Translation

A common workflow: you wrote a 250-word conference abstract, now you need a 15-minute talk. Map it:

| Abstract Section | Slides | Content |
|-----------------|--------|---------|
| Background (1-2 sentences) | 1-2 slides | Problem + gap, one compelling statistic |
| Methods (2-3 sentences) | 2-3 slides | Pipeline overview, then one slide per key method |
| Results (2-4 sentences) | 3-5 slides | One key finding per slide, assertion titles |
| Conclusions (1-2 sentences) | 1 slide | 3-4 bullet takeaways |
| — | 1 slide | Acknowledgments + QR code to paper/preprint |

Total: 8-12 slides for 15 minutes. If you have more, cut — don't speed up.

### Pattern: Capability Presentation for Technical Scoping

When presenting capabilities to a potential partner for initial scoping:

```
Structure:
1. "Who We Are" (1 slide) — company overview, NOT history
2. "Relevant Capabilities" (3-5 slides) — ONLY capabilities relevant 
   to THIS partner's problem, with evidence
3. "How We've Solved Similar Problems" (2-3 slides) — case studies,
   sanitized if necessary, with quantitative outcomes
4. "What We'd Propose" (1-2 slides) — high-level scope, NOT detailed SOW
5. "Discussion" (1 slide) — questions to understand their needs better

Total: 8-12 slides for a 30-min meeting (leaves 15+ min for discussion)
```

The critical mistake: presenting your ENTIRE capability set instead of curating for relevance. A genomics company doesn't need to see your fermentation capabilities (unless the project involves both).
