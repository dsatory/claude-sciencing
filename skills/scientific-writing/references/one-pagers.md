# One-Pagers and Executive Summaries

The one-pager is the most brutally compressed format in scientific communication. Your reader has 60-90 seconds. Every word, number, and design choice must justify its existence.

## When to Use

- Executive briefing before a decision point
- Pre-meeting context for a partner or funder
- Program summary for non-technical stakeholders
- Technology overview for business development
- Quick-reference summary accompanying a longer document

## Structure (One Page, ~400-600 words)

### Header
- Title (bold, descriptive)
- Subtitle or tagline (one line)
- Date and author/team

### The Hook (1-2 sentences)
Open with the most compelling fact, number, or question. Not background — impact.

**Example:** "The US imports >90% of its 1,3-propanediol at $1.8B/year, yet domestic fermentation routes remain uncompetitive above $2.20/kg. Our engineered *E. coli* platform produces 1,3-PDO from crude glycerol at $1.45/kg MSP — cost-competitive with petrochemical routes."

### The Problem (2-3 sentences)
Why this matters. Use a specific number.

### The Solution (3-5 sentences)
What you're doing and why it's different. One level of technical detail — enough to be credible, not enough to lose the reader.

### Key Data (featured element)
A single figure, table, or set of 3-4 highlighted metrics. This is the visual center of the page.

For example, a callout box:
> **Key Metrics**
> - 152 g/L titer in fed-batch (2.3× previous best reported)
> - 1.1 g/g yield on crude glycerol (96% of theoretical maximum)
> - MSP: $1.45/kg (competitive with $1.50-2.20/kg petrochemical)

### Path Forward / The Ask (2-3 sentences)
What you need (funding, partnership, decision) or what happens next.

### Footer
Contact information, confidentiality marking, program reference

## Design Principles

- **White space is information.** A cramped one-pager reads like none of it matters. Generous margins and spacing signal confidence in the content.
- **One figure maximum.** A process diagram or comparison chart. No decorative graphics.
- **Bullet points over paragraphs** where possible.
- **Bold key numbers** so a skimming reader catches them.
- **No jargon.** If the reader needs a glossary, you've lost them.

## Common Mistakes

- Trying to fit the full proposal into one page (result: unreadable 8pt text)
- Leading with background instead of impact
- No clear ask or next step
- Missing the audience — too technical for executives, too vague for scientists
- Including references (a one-pager is not a scholarly document)
